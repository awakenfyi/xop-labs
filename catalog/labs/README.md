# xOPs in the Wild

**Operating rules I'm developing through real work with AI.**

I'm a CMO, not a developer, and I've never claimed to be one. These are operating rules I'm building
and testing while doing actual work — brand, design, content, strategy, leadership, communication.

They started as corrections I kept repeating:

- Don't force the content into a familiar template.
- Don't reopen a decision without new evidence.
- Don't call something complete before it's been checked.
- Don't optimize conversion by weakening the brand.
- Don't present taste as an objective design rule.

Instead of correcting the model again and again, I'm turning those judgments into **xOPs** — reusable
rules that state when they apply, when they should change, and what they must never override.

These are experiments, not standards. Some will become useful team practices. Some will fail. Some
will turn out to be personal preferences rather than real xOPs. I'm publishing the process —
including the misses — so the distinction gets clearer through use.

## What "in the wild" means (and doesn't)

"In the wild" describes **where** I'm working on these — in real projects, not a lab bench — and the
**posture**: open, unfinished, tested by use. It does **not** describe how validated they are.

Two separate axes, kept separate on purpose:

**Adoption** *(where a rule is in its life)*
`Field Note → Lab xOP → Team xOP → Reference xOP`
- *Field Note* — a failure I noticed in real work.
- *Lab xOP* — a candidate rule I'm actively trying, under my own review.
- *Team xOP* — adopted for a specific org or workflow.
- *Reference xOP* — reusable across contexts, with stronger evidence behind it.

**Evidence** *(how much proof there is)*
`DESIGNED → EVALUATION-READY → RULE-TESTED → HUMAN-EVALUATED → FIELD-VALIDATED`

Everything here is a **Lab xOP**, and almost everything here is **`DESIGNED`** — it has a coherent
shape and an evaluation plan. That is not the same as proven effective, independently validated, or
shown to work beyond my own desk.

## What you'll find here

- **Field Notes** — recurring failures I notice while working with AI.
- **Lab xOPs** — candidate operating rules I'm actively testing.
- **Work Packs** — combinations of Skills, xOPs, Guards, and tests for a real role or project.
- **Receipts** — where a rule helped, where it overcorrected, and where I changed or retired it.

## How to read this

Treat every entry as a working hypothesis:

```
Observed in real work
        ↓
Written as a candidate xOP
        ↓
Tested against examples and opposite errors
        ↓
Revised, adopted, or retired
```

Every entry carries this label, so it's never mistaken for a finished standard:

```
IN THE WILD · LAB xOP · DESIGNED
Used in real work by its author. Not independently validated. Human review required.
```

## The Labs aren't a junk drawer

A correction earns a place here only if: it recurred in real work (or is consequential enough to
capture once) · the right behavior changes with observable conditions · a valid exception exists ·
the opposite error matters · it isn't just a fixed Guard or a how-to Skill · and someone owns
reviewing, revising, or retiring it. There's no universal pass mark — each Lab xOP carries its own
evaluation plan and its own opposite-error control.

## First lab: Design Lab

The operating rules I'm developing for brand, design, and conversion work. An xOP won't give a model
taste — that stays human judgment. It can stop the model from overriding my taste without a reason,
reverting to generic SaaS patterns, reopening approved decisions, forcing content into the wrong
template, or trading brand integrity for clicks. Five I'm trying:

- **Content Before Template** — the layout follows the content hierarchy and user task, not the other way around.
- **Hierarchy Follows the Decision** — visual prominence tracks the user's decision and the priority, not the most dramatic element.
- **Critique With Evidence** — a design critique cites an observable basis (task, hierarchy, brand principle, accessibility, system rule), never taste-as-defect.
- **Reuse Before Reinvention** — use an approved component while it meets the requirement; only depart when it genuinely can't.
- **Conversion Without Manipulation** — persuasion reflects real value and truthful urgency; no fabricated scarcity or disguised choices.

(Design splits cleanly: *how to build a concept* is a Skill · *contrast, tokens, breakpoints* are
Guards · *when to preserve, challenge, reuse, or depart* is the xOP · *whether it's beautiful* stays
mine. That mix is what makes design a good Lab domain.)

## The invitation

You don't need to be a developer to build an xOP. You need to know your work well enough to notice:

1. what the AI keeps getting wrong;
2. when that same behavior would actually be right;
3. what evidence should change the decision;
4. what must never be lost in the correction.

That's what I'm working on here. *Notice the correction. Name the valid exception. Test both
directions. Keep what works.*
